Sweden – the global role model of fighting climate change

With a share of 60.1% of renewable energies in 2020, Sweden is one of the most climate friendly countries in the world. For comparison, Germany’s share of renewable energies amounted to 16.4% in the same year. Furthermore, almost 50% of newly admitted cars in 2021 were electric cars. Meanwhile, the share of electric cars was only about 17% in 2021 in Germany. Even though both countries are planning on achieving emission neutrality by 2045, Sweden seems to be closer to reaching said goal. But what is so different about Sweden?

Generally speaking, there are four main differences regarding Sweden’s and Germany’s approaches to fighting climate change.

No.1: Nuclear Energy

Since the 1970s, Sweden has been employing nuclear reactors to produce electricity for commercial use. As of today, four nuclear power plants are still in use, producing about 30% of the country’s electricity. While they may produce radioactive waste, nuclear power plants produce no carbon emissions, making them a climate friendly and reliable source of energy. In Germany however, all 17 nuclear power stations had been shut off by the end of 2021 as a response to the Fukushima disaster of 2011. As a result, Germany still heavily relies on fossil fuels that emit high levels of CO2. While opinions may differ about the safety of nuclear reactors, their role as a low-carbon energy source cannot be questioned.

No.2: Hydro Energy

In the Swedish electricity system, hydro power is currently Sweden's largest source of renewable energy and accounts for approximately 45% of Swedish electricity generation. This is only possible due to Sweden’s naturally good availability of watercourses in forms of lakes and rivers. This is not possible in Germany as Germany simply does not have as good access to water as Sweden has. However, the German wind energy industry is steadily growing as its access to the Baltic Sea and especially the north of Germany make great options for reliable wind farms.

No.3: Carbon Tax

As one of the first countries in the world, Sweden introduced a carbon tax in 1991. Ever since, the price per ton of CO2 has risen from about 30€ in 1991 to approximately 120€ in 2021. Additionally, the taxes on fossil motor fuels and oil for heating are especially high in Sweden, encouraging households to switch to cars and heating powered by electricity. While Germany also taxes fossil motor fuels and oil for heating, the carbon tax was only introduced in 2021 with a current price of 25€ per ton. Therefore, there is a lot more financial pressure on Swedish companies to start using more green energy in order to avoid high taxes. As a result, Sweden’s carbon emissions have decreased by over 40% since 1990.

No.4: The Swedish Public Transport System

Due to its high share of renewable energies, many public transport systems in Sweden are powered by green energy. However, this is not the only thing that makes the Swedish public transport system different from Germany’s public transport system. While only about 14% of the German population use public transport frequently, this number is doubled when it comes to Sweden. So, what makes the Swedish public transport system so attractive?

Taking a closer look at the Swedish Transport System – Stockholm County

Stockholm County is the area around and including Stockholm, the capital and largest city of Sweden with an approximate of 980.000 inhabitants. With its more than two million inhabitants, Stockholm County is the most densely populated county in Sweden and the largest urban area in Scandinavia. Consequently, a reliable and effective public transport system is of extreme importance.

Key Traits of the Stockholm Public Transport (SL)

- Variety: Stockholm Metro (Tunnelbana), commuter trains, light rail and even driverless busses
- Simplicity: all SL services use the same integrated ticket system
- Online Accessibility: the SL website contains all ticket and transport information as well as a journey planner
- Availability:

• The Stockholm Metro runs from 05:00 to 01:00 on weekdays, as well as being replaced by night busses from 01:00 to 05:00
• Trains run around the clock on weekends
• Busses serve all areas the metro does not reach: they operate every 3-10 minutes in the city and every 7-20 minutes in the less populated areas

- Modernity: the Stockholm Metro is known for its outstanding visual art at stations and trains, making travelling by train a safer and more comfortable experience

Conclusion of the Case Study

Sweden can serve as a role model to Germany not only in broader terms when it comes to climate protection, but especially when it comes improving the German public transport system. As Germany currently opposes the idea of reactivating its nuclear power stations, making its public transport system more attractive could be a first step towards emission neutrality.
As seen in the comparison with the Swedish transport system, some key aspects that would require improvement in Germany include a higher frequency, better online access and especially improvement of train and bus stations to ensure both passengers’ safety as well as simply a feeling of comfort and hygiene when taking the local public transport.
